Masanet 2024–2026: 50 Headline Statistics on Data-Center Energy, Carbon, and Water

Sourced from Eric Masanet's most recent co-authored work: the LBNL 2024 United States Data Center Energy Usage Report (Shehabi, Smith, Hubbard, Newkirk, Lei, Siddik, Holecek, Koomey, Masanet, Sartor, LBNL, Dec 2024) and Lei, Lu, Shehabi & Masanet (2025), "The water use of data center workloads," Resources, Conservation and Recycling 219, 108310. Where useful, direct-quoted commentary from Masanet's 2025 Bren School interview is included. Numbers below are all cited to the sentence that states them.

⚡ U.S. data center electricity — historical and near-term totals

1

U.S. data center annual energy use stayed flat at ~60 TWh from 2014–2016, continuing a minimal-growth trend that had held since roughly 2010 [^1].

2

By 2018 U.S. data centers consumed ~76 TWh, or 1.9% of total U.S. electricity, with the uptick starting in 2017 as GPU-accelerated AI servers took a meaningful share of the stock [^1].

3

In 2023, U.S. data centers reached 176 TWh, 4.4% of total U.S. electricity consumption [^1].

4

Sector-wide electricity use grew at a compound annual rate of ~7% (2014–2018), accelerating to 18% (2018–2023) [^1].

5

The report's 2028 scenarios span ~325–580 TWh of annual data-center electricity use (low/high) [^1].

6

That translates to a 74–132 GW total power demand for data centers by 2028 [^1].

7

The 2028 range is 6.7%–12.0% of total forecast U.S. electricity consumption — up from ~4% today [^1].

8

For scale: the 2023 176 TWh is comparable to the electricity used by approximately 6.7 million average U.S. households [^1].

🖥️ AI servers as the dominant driver

9

Between 2014 and 2023, total annual server electricity use more than tripled, from roughly 30 TWh to nearly 100 TWh (with AI servers explaining most of the growth after 2017) [^1].

10

AI-accelerated (GPU) server energy grew from <2 TWh in 2017 to >40 TWh in 2023 [^1].

11

When the previous LBNL report was published in 2016, AI servers were only ~2% of total server energy — a share that has since exploded [^2].

12

Masanet estimates AI servers use up to 10× the power of a standard server, and companies are deploying them at unprecedented scale [^3].

13

Larger-scale AI configurations are already in the pipeline: NVIDIA's 72-GPU rack-scale GB200 has been announced, with expected power draw scaling near-linearly from 8-GPU machines [^1].

14

Empirical benchmarking showed 8×H100 GPU nodes averaged 74% of manufacturer-rated TDP across single- and multi-node AI training workloads [^1].

15

For 2024–2028, the report varies AI-server operational power between 60% and 80% of rated power to reflect future operating uncertainty [^1].

16

AI-server idle power is assumed at ~20% of rated maximum, based on measured 8-GPU H100 nodes drawing ~18% of manufacturer max at idle [^1].

17

In 2023, inference accounted for nearly 60% of AI server energy — but by 2028, training is projected to overtake it, consuming 50–53% of AI-server energy as more high-TDP GPUs are allocated to training runs [^1].

18

New AI campuses are being built with individual power capacities up to ~1 gigawatt — enough to power all homes in Philadelphia [^2].

🏗️ Structural shifts: where the servers live

19

In 2014, internal (enterprise) data centers housed 24% of servers; by 2020, hyperscale+colocation combined jumped from 20% (2014) to 57% (2020) of servers [^1].

20

In 2014, over 60% of server energy consumption was in internal data centers; by 2023 that share fell to nearly 10%, with hyperscale and colocation together accounting for almost 80% [^1].

21

By 2028, internal DCs' share of server energy falls below 2%, and hyperscale+colo exceed 90%, primarily driven by AI workloads [^1].

22

Amazon, Microsoft, and Google collectively own more than half of hyperscale data centers [^1].

23

Virginia hosts the highest data-center electricity demand in the U.S., followed by California and Texas [^1].

24

In Northern Virginia, DC concentration has pushed utilities to keep fossil-fuel plants online to meet demand [^3].

25

Masanet notes renewable-energy deployment is not scaling fast enough to match AI growth; nuclear and geothermal are being explored but are "not yet widely available" [^3].

🌡️ Efficiency: PUE and site WUE

26

PUE = total data-center electricity ÷ IT-equipment electricity (dimensionless kWh/kWh); WUE = total water ÷ IT-equipment electricity, reported in liters per kWh [^1].

27

The report ran over 1 million PUE/WUE simulations across cooling types, climates, and operating regimes [^1].

28

Annual average PUE fell from ~1.6 in 2014 to ~1.4 in 2023 [^1].

29

By 2028, average PUE is projected to fall to 1.15–1.35, driven by the continued shift to hyperscale/colo [^1].

30

Average site WUE stayed just above 0.36 L/kWh through 2023 [^1].

31

Post-2023, average site WUE actually rises to 0.45–0.48 L/kWh as more evaporative and adiabatic cooling is deployed to compensate for higher heat loads [^1].

32

Best-in-class hyperscale facilities self-report WUE as low as 0.1–0.3 L/kWh for adiabatic-cooling configurations — better than the modeled aggregate [^1].

💧 Direct water consumption

33

U.S. data centers directly consumed ~21.2 billion liters of water in 2014, of which 64% was in internal data centers [^1].

34

Lei/Masanet (2025) update that figure: U.S. data centers directly consumed ~66 billion liters in 2023, a threefold rise in a decade [^4].

35

The LBNL 2024 report projects U.S. data centers will consume 60–124 billion liters of direct water by 2028 [^1].

36

Direct water use covers space humidification, direct adiabatic cooling, and heat rejection in cooling towers — evaporation, windage loss, and blowdown/draw-off water [^4].

37

As a local-impact example, Google's data centers in The Dalles, Oregon consumed 355 million gallons of water in 2021 — 29% of the city's total water consumption during regional drought [^4].

🏭 Indirect (electricity-embedded) water and emissions

38

Total indirect water footprint of U.S. data centers is ~800 billion liters — more than 10× direct water use — attributable to electricity-generation water consumption at the regional grid mix [^1].

39

Associated 2023 GHG emissions from data-center electricity are ~61 billion kg CO₂-equivalent at the current grid mix [^1].

40

Combustion of fossil fuels (coal, gas, petroleum) accounts for >99% of GHG emissions from electricity generation [^1].

41

1 kWh of thermoelectric generation embeds 0.18–2.0 L of water, an order-of-magnitude range depending on generator technology [^4].

42

1 kWh of hydropower embeds 0.67–1,194 L — the reservoir-evaporation extremes are what make indirect water use so location-dependent [^4].

📊 Workload-level water use (Lei/Masanet 2025)

43

Workload-level data-center water use varies by more than 10,000× across configurations, driven by ~1,000× differences in water per kWh of grid electricity and ~10× in server workload efficiency [^4].

44

Determinants ranked: server efficiency → grid water factors → server utilization → cooling system type → infrastructure efficiency → climate zone → inactive-server percentage → refresh cycle [^4].

45

The paper introduces a workload-normalized metric (liters per ssj_ops) using the SPECpower_ssj 2008 benchmark, allowing fair cross-DC comparisons that WUE-site and WUE-source cannot support [^4].

46

The framework is explicitly extensible to AI workloads via MLPerf — making it the methodological bridge from Masanet's earlier PUE/WUE work to the GenAI era [^4].

47

There is "no silver bullet" for water reduction — optimal outcomes require tailored combinations of renewable-electricity sourcing, high-efficiency servers, high utilization via virtualization, and site-appropriate cooling [^4].

💾 Storage and networking (often overlooked)

48

Storage electricity use grows from 7 TWh in 2014 to 16 TWh in 2023 to 22 TWh by 2028; the flash share climbs from 6% to 25% to 50% over the same period, while HDD electricity use peaks at 12.3 TWh in 2023 and then declines [^1].

49

Network equipment electricity reaches ~23 TWh by 2028, with InfiniBand switches jumping from just under 10% (2023) to 45% (2028) of total network electricity — an AI-training-cluster fingerprint [^1].

🎯 Framing statistic

50

Masanet's own framing of the AI era: "Even with efficiency gains, AI's energy footprint is still expected to grow. The key is ensuring that this growth aligns with sustainable energy deployment rather than exacerbating fossil-fuel dependence" [^3].
